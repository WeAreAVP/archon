Documentation

Playback carries with it the risk that your tape could be irrevocably damaged by the playback and/or that the playback will cause damage to the playback machine. For this reason we don't recommend playback as a means of assessment; however, a strictly visual inspection of a tape will not provide all the clues about the tape's sound/picture quality. If this item has been played back recently there may be notes indicating the playback quality, that is, if there was anything worth noting.

If you have any past documentation about playback or tape quality, this information can be extremely useful in assessing the tape without having to subject it to playback risks. Additionally, documentation may provide evidence of past preservation measures taken on the content or item. You should always document any preservation measures that you take on your materials. This provides evidence to future preservationists and researchers about the path that the content/object has traveled.

Your documentation should NOT be stored with the item. You should store your documentation (for example, notes written on paper) in a separate physical location. Paper has numerous conservation issues and can be more damaging than helpful. For example, paper can damage your tape by shedding fibers which can abrade the tape surface. If you track your items via electronic means, you may be able to note preservation history in your electronic tracking system.

Having additional documentation about your item can help inform your future preservation decisions.
