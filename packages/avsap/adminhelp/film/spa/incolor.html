Black and White or Color

Film Color At-A-Glance

This question may involve unspooling the film to answer. We recommend doing this only after you've looked at our "Film Handling Basics" section. Unspooling film is not without risk. If you are unable or unwilling to unspool your film and you have no supporting information which may answer this question, we recommend you click on "I Don't Know."

  • Black and white film is at lower preservation priority
  • Color film is at higher preservation priority
  • Black and white negative film often has a greyish or purplish cast
  • Color negative film often has an orange cast
  • The easiest way to determine if a film is in color is to unspool it and look at a few frames.
  • If you have a film that has some parts in color and some in black and white be sure to click on "color/both"

Black and white motion picture film contains silver salt particles which are suspended in a gelatin binder. During processing, these salts are converted to metallic silver particles, which is what the image on the film is composed of.
