Film - Appropriate Container

Optimal appropriate film containers:

  • protect the film from damage, dust, and other contaminants
  • do not exacerbate film deterioration—dirty, rusty, non-vented, dented, metal containers can heighten the potential for film surface damage
  • particularly in the case of acetate films, a good canister allows a film to "breathe"; that is, the canister is vented to allow for air exchange

Ideally, motion picture film should be stored in vented plastic cans. Film stored in metal containers or stored without a container will have a higher preservation priority.

Film without a container is especially vulnerable to damage or contamination—surface scratching, unwinding, dust and mold. According to the NFPF Film Preservation Guide, "Film containers—boxes or cans—should be convenient to use and should protect the film from dust and physical damage" (NFPF, p. 66).

If your film is stored in a rusty, dirty container it is not receiving adequate protection. Dirt and rust flakes can scratch the delicate film surface. You should consider replacing damaged containers in order to increase the longevity of your film.

Containers are made from a variety of materials; inert plastics, non-corrosive metal and cardboard. The container your institution uses will largely depend upon the film storage environment. Are you storing your film in a standard room-temperature environment? Are they stored in a freezer? If you are storing your film in a room-temperature environment, polypropylene or polyethylene containers which are vented are optimal. This will allow for the film to "breathe" and to off-gas vapors that may be emitted from the film. If you are storing your film in freezing temperatures, your cans should be sealed and moisture-resistant. In either case, cans should be clean and free from debris, rust and structural damage (NFPF, 2004).

When freezing film ensure that the the film is properly housed by double-bagging it in zip-sealed freezer bags. If you are interested in freezing infrequently accessed materials refer to The Home Film Preservation Guide, sec. 8 ( www.filmforever.org) for detailed steps on how prepare your film for long-term freezing (NFPF, 2004).
