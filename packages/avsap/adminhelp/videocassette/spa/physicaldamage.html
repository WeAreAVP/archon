Cartridge and Cassette-Based Video - Physical Damage

In order to inspect the tape, you will have to be able to see the tape. To this end, you will have to move the tape shield out of the way. The tape shield protects the tape from dust and material and from being handled. Playback decks move the shield out of the way so that the tape can come into contact with the playback heads in the machine. You can usually move the shield out of the way yourself in order to inspect the tape. Moving the tape shield is usually very easy to do. Following are two pictures of a VHS tape, one with the shield in place, and the next with the shield moved out of the way in order to expose the tape for inspection.

If your videocassette is in the fully rewound or fast-forwarded position—which is recommended—you may see tape leader when you open the tape shield. Leader is generally made of uncoated polyester tape and is used to protect the tape. As tape is played back, it is most likely to be damaged at the beginning and end of the film. If there are any issues with the playback equipment, the leader would ideally take the brunt of the damage. Damaged leader is easier to replace than unique sections of tape.

If you load a videocasette with crumpled up or otherwise damaged videotape into a deck, you're asking for trouble because you're significantly increasing the odds of the tape being eaten and/or further damaged by the playback machine. (Sadly, there are no guarantees that even a pristine tape won't be eaten by a machine—this is why we must preserve playback equipment alongside our media.)
