Mold

MOLD CAN BE TOXIC. Proceed with caution when examining tapes for mold. Do not handle moldy tapes longer than necessary. If you find a moldy tape place it in a sealable plastic bag until conservation action can be taken. To check for mold on a videocassette:

  • Inspect the tape container. If you see fuzzy brown, black, white, green or mustard patches this is most likely mold. Mold on the container is a good indicator that the mold has traveled to or from the cassette. If you suspect mold, immediately seal the tape into a plastic bag.
  • Look through the clear plastic windows on the cassette, or any other area which allows you to see the tape pack. Look for black, brown, white, green or mustard colored fuzzy patches. If you see any, you're most likely looking at mold.
  • Carefully flip open the tape shield. How the tape shield opens differs between videocassettes; however, you are generally looking for a button or tab on the short edge of the cassette generally near the tape shield. See: http://www.arts.state.tx.us/video/assess.asp for additional tape handling, and inspection guidance including how to open a tape shield. Again, look for for black, brown, white, green or mustard colored fuzzy patches or fibers at the edge of the tape. It can be very hard to assess mold on videocassettes, largely because you can't see very well into the cassette at the tape itself.

Tapes suspected of being moldy should NOT be played back as the mold can get into the playback machinery and cause poor playback. Mold can also travel onto other tapes so it is best to quarantine any tapes suspected of being moldy. It should also be noted that exposure to mold is very bad for humans, so your moldy tapes (and tapes suspected of being moldy) should be put inside sealed plastic bags (Texas, 2007). Mold is very difficult to clean off of video and audiotapes, particularly cassette-based tapes, so you're going to have to send these tapes on to professionals for thorough cleaning. This isn't something you're going to want to do in-house (Wheeler, 2002, p. 12).
