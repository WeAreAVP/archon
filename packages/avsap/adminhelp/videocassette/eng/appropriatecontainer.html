Cartridge and Cassette-Based Video - Storage Container

When storing videocassettes and cartridges, it is best to keep them in inert plastic containers (Texas, 2007). This helps to keep airborne contaminants as well as pests off of the videotapes. There are several preservation supply companies that design and sell cases for the more ubiquitous cassette formats such as VHS and mini-DV. The container for a videotape should be clean and should provide some protection for the recording surface. Moldy, damaged and dirty containers should be replaced with clean ones.

If your videocassette is housed in a plastic container and you're unsure if it is inert or is acceptable for preservation purposes, check the condition of the container. If it is protecting the tape from dust, pests and other contaminants, not shedding or introducing any contaminants through its own degradation, and is clean and free of mold or excessive dirt, the container is acceptable for use. Rehousing to a more expensive inert plastic container is not vital in this case. If the tape is housed in anything other than a hard plastic container, e.g. in a cardboard slipcover, or is not sufficiently protected, it should be rehoused in an inert plastic container.
