Access Copies

One of the hallmarks of audiovisual preservation is that we strive to preserve originals of the media object. Here, "original" refers to the earliest generation of an audiovisual object. In some cases, it will come right out of the capturing device—for example, film or videotape from a camera. In many instances, however, an institution will not know if it is holding the "original." For example, you may have a 16mm film and have no idea if it is the earliest version. In this case, you must assume that it is the original and treat it as such.

Successive iterations of an audiovisual object tend to have degrading picture and sound quality—which is all the more reason to actively preserve the originals. Ideally, the originals should be used once to make an "access copy." The access copy would then be put to use by the institution in place of the original. For example, if your institution has a 16mm film, the best policy would be to transfer the film and allow the duplicate to be the viewing copy for the public. This would save the original film from the wear and tear of use.
