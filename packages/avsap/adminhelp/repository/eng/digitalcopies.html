Digital Copies

Digitization is the process by which digital copies of analog audiovisual materials are made. Because of the impending obsolescence of analog formats, it is clear that digitization will become more and more essential. Additionally, making high quality digital copies for access purposes spares the originals from possible damage. However, a digitization program can be costly for an institution. This is one key reason to preserve your analog originals for as long as possible. Because digitization also takes time and effort, it is best to plan to digitize your materials over time, as part of your ongoing preservation workflow.

Digital formats have evolved quickly in a short period of time and continue to evolve. For this reason, we cannot recommend particular digital formats. However, there are things to keep in mind when choosing to digitize your AV materials:

  • First, you will need a playback machine for each format you wish to digitize.
  • Second, it is best to use non-proprietary digital formats, where possible. Since proprietary formats are owned by companies, if they are no longer supported, you could be stuck with a great number of digital objects and no software to play them with. You could be trading obsolete analog originals for obsolete digital surrogates.
  • Third, it is best to digitize your materials using uncompressed formats. Compression discards information from a file in order to make it smaller and more manageable. This allows sound and video files to be more accessible and easily shared online. The problem with compression, however, is that as information is discarded, it is lost from the digital copy; it is not possible to recover the information. For example, when a video is digitized and compressed, some color information (along with other information) is discarded to maintain a small file size. This color information is then not included in the compressed digital copy and cannot be recovered. For best practices when digitizing an audiovisual original, use uncompressed formats to make a digital master , which is the highest-quality digital copy possible. You can then make smaller, compressed digital "access copies" from the digital master.
  • Last but not least, it is important to remember that digital copies must also be preserved, which is no easy task. Digital objects face obsolescence at least as much as analog objects. As digital media evolve, older formats are sometimes discarded and can become difficult to support. For example, were you to have a WordStar document today, it would be hard to find a way to access that document. AvSAP does not treat digital preservation, since it is a vast and burgeoning field. For our purposes here it should suffice to say that as you create digital copies of your analog originals, you will have to take steps to preserve those digital copies. Researching storage systems and digital formats will be critical to the long-term preservation of your digital materials.
