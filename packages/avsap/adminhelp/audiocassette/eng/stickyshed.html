Sticky Shed Syndrome (Binder Hydrolysis)

Magnetic tape is made of at least three layers. The first is the plastic tape layer, which is the base upon which the other layers are supported. The topmost layer is the recording layer, which contains the metal particles that carry recorded information. Between these two layers is a binder layer, which keeps the recording layer adhered to the base layer. Sticky shed syndrome, also known as binder hydrolysis, is a situation in which the binder layer has taken on moisture from the environment and starts to break down. Sticky shed is very common and can be difficult to diagnose by eye. It can be present as a whitish powder on the tape, but it can also be invisible. During playback, sticky shed can cause the tape to squeal and can distort the audio and/or video. Playing back a tape suffering from sticky shed syndrome can cause the binder material to flake off and gum up the heads and other parts of your playback deck.

Almost all magnetic tape can suffer from sticky shed syndrome. This is an example of inherent vice, a situation in which the very design of an information recording medium carries within it the seeds of its own demise. Some brands of audio or videotape are more susceptible to sticky shed than others; however, we must do our best to control and monitor the storage environment for all of our magnetic media to slow down incidences of sticky shed syndrome. Controlling humidity levels is essential for preventing the syndrome. Variances in humidity and temperature should always be avoided, as these variances can also prematurely age materials. Generally speaking, it is best to move audiovisual materials from the attic, shed, basement or other environment that does not maintain constant temperature and relative humidity throughout the year. According to the Library of Congress (2002), magnetic tapes should be stored at 45 to 50 degrees F and 20 to 30% relative humidity (RH). Storing tape at below 45% RH is not advisable as it can break down the lubrication that is built into the tape. While film likes to be frozen for long-term storage, magnetic tape does not. Many experts agree that controlling the humidity in which magnetic tapes are stored is more important than controlling the temperature (Clark, 2002).

Remember that magnetic tapes that squeal during playback, show powdery or flaky material on the tape, or have been stored in very humid conditions could be suffering from sticky shed syndrome and should not be played back. Such tapes can in some cases be temporarily treated by being baked at low levels (like dehydrating food). When a tape is baked successfully, it can usually be played back one time, which is usually enough to transfer the information off of the bad tape and onto a new medium. While baking can be very effective, we do not recommend you try to do this yourself. Instead, contact a professional audiovisual restoration facility. Only open reel audio tapes can be treated in this manner. DO NOT attempt this with audio cassettes.
