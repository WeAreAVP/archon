Average Humidity

Humidity, along with temperature, is one of the most important and potentially damaging factors in your storage environment. Maintaining a stable relative humidity can help increase the viable lives of your audiovisual media. Changes in humidity can cause AV items to swell and contract, encourage the growth of mold, and speed up or even cause chemical decay. The following are the relative humidity standards for several format types:

  • Motion picture film (color or black and white) should be stored between 30% and 50% relative humidity. Storing it below 30% will cause the film to get too dry and lose its suppleness. Film must be flexible in order to travel through projectors and printing machines. If it gets too dry it can crack and break. Storing acetate film and nitrate film above 50% can increase acetate decay (vinegar syndrome) and nitrate decay, respectively. All film bases stored at about 50% relative humidity become more susceptible to mold infestation.
  • Magnetic video and audiotape should also be stored between 30% and 50% relative humidity. If magnetic tape gets too dry, its lubricant can freeze and the tape will have difficulty moving through playback equipment. If tape is stored above 50% relative humidity, it can become more susceptible to sticky shed syndrome and mold.
  • Grooved audio media should be stored at a relative humidity around 45% (Warren, 1993, p. 138)
  • Optical media like CDs and DVDs should be stored at a relative humidity between 30% and 50%. Relative humidity levels above 50% can cause corrosion and mold growth.

Keeping your audiovisual materials between 30% and 50% relative humidity is ideal. It is important to remember, however, that often when you lower the temperature, you raise the relative humidity. This is why it is critical to monitor the humidity and temperature in your storage facilities. It is best to keep your media from wide variances in temperature and relative humidity, even if you can't completely control both of these factors. It is also good to keep your media away from extremes—very low or very high humidity. Bringing materials out of uncontrolled environments like basements, sheds, and attics and moving them into climate controlled areas like internal rooms or even closets can do much to keep these materials viable.
